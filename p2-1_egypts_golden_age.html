(2:1) Part Two: Egypt’s Golden Age

And God said to Abram, Know well that your offspring shall be strangers in a land not theirs, and they shall be enslaved and oppressed four hundred years.

Gen 15:13.


And they shall return here in the fourth generation, for the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete.

Gen 15:16.
The Jewish Study Bible. Tanakh Translation. Oxford University Press 1985.

The ancient concept of time versus opportunity.

The length of the Israelites’ sojourn has been cause for debate amongst scholars for some time, and was one that this series wrestled with. The Biblical text, such as the Genesis examples above, often seems to contradict itself and different traditions appear to interpret the text in different ways. For example, in using the passage above, the birth of the child that fathered each of the subsequent generations would have to be born in his fathers old age, if four generations were to span four hundred years. Also, some traditions state the sojourn started with the entry of Abram into Egypt, Canaan and Goshen, while others state it began with Jacob’s entry into Egypt. My solution to this problem is that scholars debate it, faith walkers surrender.

But this interpretation in itself, that of moving these statements of time from the linear measured ‘chronos’ of the historian, to that of the appointed time of the religious, ‘kairos’, can be a source of conflict and dissatisfaction for those seeking to affirm or reaffirm their faith. Reconciling specific historical people, locations and events to specific characters, cities and episodes of the Bible is problematic. Yet this is the form which many an investigation has taken. Indeed, this series highlights the problems with such an approach. [26]

The Sojourn of Israel.

During my research I have come to believe that we are encountering several traditions that have been brought together through common experiences. But these experiences may occupy different periods in history. As the Bible’s genre is not primarily concerned with recording history, but a conveying of God’s instructions, these experiences seldom conform to the chronology that the western genre of history writing follows. These experiences are as relevant to today’s society as they were to the generations of the past. But through the passage of time, our common values have changed and so our ability to understand the Genesis, Exodus tradition’s message has been impaired.

To be able to reconstruct the Genesis and Exodus text it is therefore necessary to gain a contextual understanding within which the text was written. The Joseph, Moses and Egypt's Three Kingdoms series of three programmes has been produced in order to give such an introduction and should be viewed alongside this narrative. Hopefully, together, these will provide a springboard to further personal insight and growth.

The Threat to Egypt.

Let us deal shrewdly with them, so that they may not increase; otherwise in the event of war they may join our enemies in fighting against us and rise from the ground.

Exodus 1:10.
The Jewish Study Bible. Tanakh Translation. Oxford University Press 1985.

Previously it was suggested that the Hebrews may have been charged with supplying food to the royal palace. While these tent dwellers and their flocks may have remained autonomous, it is probable that a portion of their herd would have been given in exchange for Goshen grazing rights. Relations between the Israelites and the Egyptians may have oscillated between cooperation and dissension, depending on the pressures of working poor scrub or marshy land and the number of sheep and goats exchanged to pay for it. This is no different to any modern day business, wrestling with the pressures of manufacturing, marketing, selling the product and paying business tax to the state.

The Bible states that God had promised Abram to give his descendants a land after a period of enslavement abroad. We have already noted that the dissension between pharaoh and Joseph’s kin may have started before Joseph’s death, and that the subsequent polarisation of the 2nd Intermediate Period gives us at least two possibilities for the Egyptians’ desire to “deal shrewdly with them”. If the expulsion event of the Hyksos is considered as Exodus (around 1550-1525 B.C.E.), then the Bible’s 400 year sojourn can easily accommodate the arrival of both patriarchs as the starting point for the sojourn. However, other statements within the Bible can render this hypothesis problematic.

Yet it is worth noting that the reference to the Israelites joining “our enemies” may have been a genuine expression of Egyptian fear. While the Israelites may have been an independent group, it is equally possible that they may have been in some way allied to the Canaanite/Hyksos entities vying for control of the north-eastern Delta. The possibility that they formed part of a larger coalition of similar thinkers, whose origins were from the same region as that of the Hyksos, is a strong one. Any alliance may have encouraged others from the north to come down and enter Egypt, in order to support the Canaanite/Hyksos regime change.

The mace handle of Hotepibre, from the tomb of the Lord of the Goats, Ebla, Syria. Illustration: by Khruner.

This point is further supported by early references to ‘Israel’, coming from Ebla, a thousand years before Merneptah’s Stele made reference to it in 1209 B.C.E. The word ‘Israel’ seems to have originated as a West Semitic personal name. By the time of Merneptah, it had become a larger body of people. We have already noted the possible origins for the tribe of Benjamin (Benu Yamina), and so Israel may have been just one of many names that developed into the name of an ancestor clan, tribe and finally a nation. [27] [28]

Excavations at Ebla have uncovered a mace head from the ‘Tomb of the Lord of the Goats,’ upon which a mounted hieroglyphic inscription made reference to an obscure pharaoh, Hotepibre, who ruled around 1790 B.C.E. It has been suggested that this king was of Asiatic extraction, that he may have ruled from Avaris, and that he maintained relations with Ebla. However, the artefact remains problematic due to discrepancies in the hieroglyphic inscription and many scholars argue that the possible Egyptian contact with Ebla rests on very meagre evidence. [29] [30]

What is beyond doubt, is that Hammurabi had embarked on his conquest to control the lands north and south of Babylon, which resulted in the unification of Mesopotamia under his rule in around 1750 B.C.E. This highlights the rise of the Amorites as referenced in Genesis, and it contrasts with Egypt’s opposite experience of decline and division, which culminated with the 2nd Intermediate Period.

Whether these tribal connections with Israel were perceived or real, the tensions described can fit equally well into a mid 13th or early 18th Dynasty setting and so the Egyptians’ statement of concern is understandable.

Abram the Hebrew.

The king of Egypt spoke to the Hebrew midwives, one of whom was named Shiphrah and the other Puah, saying, “when you deliver the Hebrew women, look at the birthstool; if it is a boy, kill him, if it is a girl, let her live.

Exodus 1:15-16.

I should say here, that I am using ‘Israel’ as a reference to the direct descendants of any of the sons (possible coalition tribes) of the patriarchs Abraham, Isaac and Jacob. In contrast, ‘Hebrews’, the one who stands on the other side, a non ethnic term, is being used for a people who are found to be on the wrong side of the Pharaoh. Some of these ‘Hebrews’ may have exited with ‘Israel’ at the time of their departure. I believe the difference between the two terms is significant and has a parallel link to Abram and Lot’s left hand, right hand division agreement. [31]

The story of Abram and Lot’s separation, found in Genesis 13, has previously been referenced, with Abram’s characteristically generous nature offering Lot the first choice of land. Their location is near Bethel, in the central hills of Canaan, and their agreement would have probably been formally marked by piling a number of stones on top of each other to mark the boundary. This is a practice which is still carried out today amongst the Transjordan Bedouin. Given that they are already at a location where Abram had previously built an altar, the altar would have served as the marker. Lot’s contrasting self interest compels him to take the best land, the Jordan valley, in which the cities of the plain were located. Abram’s subsequent rescue of Lot from the hands of Chedor-laomer is one of many Biblical statements, highlighting that Lot had chosen the wrong side.

The Bible credits a survivor of the conflict bringing the news of the rout and Lot’s abduction to “Abram the Hebrew (Avram Ha-Ivri)”. This translates literally as; Abram the one who stands on the other side. That is, ‘the other side’ of the marker. Separated from Abram, Lot is weak and despoiled. Reunited with Abram, through Abram’s heroism or willingness to cross the divide, Lot is restored. Abram is portrayed as the one who is at one with God, having crossed the divide and so Lot is restored to God.

In a similar way, the pharaoh is portrayed as speaking to the midwives of the Hebrews in order to eliminate or subdue his potential opponents. He is acting as Chedor-laomer binding Lot. The Bible states “Hebrew midwives,” not Israelite midwives, in order to differentiate between righteous Gentiles who stand on the other side of pharaoh and the ethnic tribal groups ‘Israel’. Pharaoh’s efforts are to no avail. As the Bible states “God dealt well with the midwives; and the people multiplied and increased greatly.” That is to say, the opposition to pharaoh increased greatly.

The story is a metaphor or literary illustration which is creating the setting for the next great allegorical illustration, the rescue of the baby Moses from the waters of the Nile. This is the classic story of the birth of a great leader and the creation of great nation (Noah, Sargon, etc.) as many scholars have noted. [32]

Clearly the unnamed pharaoh is undertaking something to which there was growing opposition. But what?
